The Problem with Certifications in Security

Published: 2024-12-01

I passed my OSCP in 2019. It was a useful experience — not because the certificate itself means anything, but because the process forced me to actually build the skill. Forty-eight hours in a lab, either you get shells or you don't. There's no multiple-choice safety net.

Five years later, I look at junior security job postings and I see a laundry list: CISSP, CEH, Security+, GIAC GPEN, CompTIA CySA+. I look at LinkedIn profiles and see people stacking five or six certifications, each one obtained via a test prep course that teaches to the exam format rather than to the domain.

I want to be direct about something: this is becoming a problem.

What Certifications Actually Signal

A certification is a signal. In the early 2000s, when there were fewer standardized credentials and no real talent pipeline, a CISSP or CISA was a meaningful shorthand — it meant you'd done some subset of work across the security domains and could at least speak the language.

That signal has degraded. Not because the certifications got worse (some have), but because the test preparation industry learned to reverse-engineer the signal and produce it cheaply. You can now pass the Security+ with two weeks of Udemy prep. That doesn't make you a security professional.

The OSCP still has some teeth, as does the GREM (malware) and a handful of others that are hands-on or require demonstrable output. But the majority of certs in the field test recall and pattern matching, not judgment under pressure.

Where I See the Real Damage

The problem isn't the certificates themselves — it's the signaling displacement they cause. When hiring managers use certification count as a proxy for capability, they create an incentive for candidates to optimize for the cert, not the skill.

I've interviewed candidates who couldn't explain how TCP handshakes work but had a Security+ and a CySA+. I've worked with consultants who had the CISSP but couldn't read a packet capture without a GUI holding their hand.

More importantly: I've passed on genuinely talented people who didn't have the letters because a recruiter or HR process filtered them out before they got to me. Those decisions cost organizations real capability.

What Actually Measures Skill

This is not original thinking, but it bears repeating: the things that actually measure whether someone knows security are mostly non-credentialed.

  • CTF results and writeups. These are transparent and reproducible.
  • Public code. Does the person write tools people actually use?
  • Documented research. Not polished marketing PDFs — actual technical analysis.
  • Practical interviews. Give them a machine and a problem. See what they do.
  • Bug bounty track record. This is real-world adversarial output.

None of these scale easily, which is why organizations default to certifications. Certifications are cheap to evaluate. But cheap evaluation produces low-quality selection.

A More Honest Take on Which Certs Are Worth It

I don't think all certifications are useless. Here's my honest assessment of where they add value:

OSCP / OSED / OSEP (OffSec): The most honest certs in the industry. Lab-based, time-limited, real exploitation required. Still worth pursuing for the learning experience, not just the paper.

GREM / GXPN (GIAC): Useful for niche specialization. GREM in particular has enough depth that passing it with a high score means something.

CISSP: Fine for management-track security roles where you need to speak the governance language. Not a technical credential. Stop treating it as one.

CEH: I'll be polite and say it should be replaced by better options. If you have it, don't lead with it.

Security+, CySA+, etc.: Reasonable for demonstrating baseline familiarity to non-technical stakeholders or for compliance boxes. That is their value. Do not mistake that for technical credibility.

What I'd Actually Recommend

If you're early in your career: spend 80% of your time building skills and producing visible output, 20% on credentials that open specific doors you need opened. Document your work publicly. Build a GitHub. Write a writeup for every CTF challenge you solve.

If you're hiring: add a practical component to your process. Give candidates a packet capture to analyze, a binary to review, a log file to triage. Hire for the output, not the credential list.

The field is short on people who can actually do the work. We can't afford to optimize for credentials over capability.
